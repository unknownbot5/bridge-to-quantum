# Linear Transformations or operators

Well, with objects in hand mathematicians are always interested in how to relate different objects and maps always do that job. For those of you, who need its use in quantum computing rather than being interested in these objects themselves, linear operators do have an important use case and that i such that it can be understood on a basic level.

In classical computing, you have gates, which are used to modify information the way we want. Well there is again a quantum analogue of it, and we call them quantum gates. Surprisingly they act as what we call linear transformation. Now, before we move to the definition of this object. I would like you to induct this fact in your head that in mathematics, we often use the term "homomorphism" after an object. The term "homomorphism" actually signifies that the properties of the object are respected while going through the map. A linear transformation is actually a vector space homomorphism, which means that if a map satisfies the following properties,  image of sum of vectors is actually the same as image of sum of the vectors and image of vector multiplied with a scalar is same as the scalar multiplication of image of the vector with the same scalar, then the map is a linear transformation. That was too bad to understand and hence I have written that down in equations 

A map $F: V \rightarrow W$ where $V$ and $W$ are vector spaces over a field $\mathbb{F}$ is called a linear transformation if, 
1. $\forall v \in V, w \in W, F(v+w) = F(v) + F(w)$ 
2. $\forall c \in \mathbb{F}, v \in V, F(c v) = c F(v)$ 

Notice, that we needed both the spaces to be under the same field, this is the only reason why the last property makes sense and is needed if we go by the name *vector space homomorphism* of *linear transformation*.

# Basis and dimension of a vector space

This is one of the most interesting parts of linear algebra, and it is the part that really brings matrices into the picture.

By definition, *basis* is any subset of vector space, which "spans" the vector space and is "linearly independent". Now, we will tackle both the terms and with ease. Without being too descriptive, I will directly list the two conditions, in another definition.

A subset $\mathcal{B} \subset V$ is a basis of a vector space $V$ over a field $\mathbb{F}$ if and only if:
1. $\forall v \in V, v = \sum_{\beta \in \mathcal{B}} c_\beta \beta$ where $c_\beta \in \mathbb{F}$
2. $\not \exists \{c_\beta\}_{\beta \in \mathcal{B}} \subset \mathbb{F}^{|\mathcal{B}|}$ s.t. some $c_\beta \neq 0$ and $\sum_\beta c_\beta \beta = 0$

The first condition corresponds to the phrase "spans the vector space" while the latter corresponds to "linear independence". Now, in most of the cases we will deal with $|\mathcal{B}|$ being finite, but this still may not be always true and we have a special name for the cardinality, which is *dimension of vector space*.

This itself would raise doubts as to how can be dimension same, we will provide a proof for the finite case, just here.

Although I should be rigorous about it, but its always better to try the details on your own, so here is a sketch of the proof, suppose we have two basis of cardinality $m$ and $n$, where $m < n$, then say the basis are $\beta_m$ and $\beta_n$ with cardinality $m$ and $n$ respectively. The strategy is to reduce the size of $\beta_n$ by arguing that there is a subset of $\beta_n$ which also works as a basis. Now, the way to do it, is that we can add an element of $\beta_n$ to $\beta_m$ and then argue that the resulting set is not linearly independent, after which we remove one element from it and argue that it is a suitable basis, we keep reapeating the process, replacing elements of $\beta_m$ with that of $\beta_n$ and when all the elements are of $\beta_n$ we are done as we have proved our claim, and this disproves linear independence of $\beta_n$.

Now, coming to the real point whats the actual use of basis. In context of quantum computing and even in our association with vectors in high school, we generally deal with 2 dimensional spaces. In context of qubits the standard basis is $\{|0 \rangle , |1 \rangle \}$
